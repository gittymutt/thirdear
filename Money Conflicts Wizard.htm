Having money conflicts? You’re not alone.! Statistics continue to show that financial problems cause considerable stress in marriages and often lead couples to divorce. Businesses large and small close their doors each year due to cash flow insufficiencies. The average American has approximately $15K in personal credit card debt and $33K in student loan debt, and wage rates are not increasing as fast as the rate of inflation on goods and services. Remember when banks used to pay 3% or more in interest on the money you allow them to borrow to make themselves money? You’re lucky now if you can get a full 1% interest income on those funds tied up at your bank. These details reflect the current circumstances for most people. So, forgive yourself for having conflicts. Let’s get started on minimizing the adverse effects of your cirucumstances.

Most people want more money. Financial wealth represents freedom, options and power, and who doesn’t want more of these? Yet many of us have brain patterns that keep us from accumulating money. We think it is “the root of all evil,” so we naturally resist having it and becoming evil. We believe it is scarce, so we avoid having too much and taking what someone else needs or deserves more. We might even wear poverty as a badge of honor, like Buddhist monks or Mother Teresa did. As T. Harv Eker states in Secrets of the Millionaire Mind, “you have to change the roots to change the fruits” on the tree you want to grow. Let’s look at what brain patterns might be influencing your relationship with money.

We get very significant about our money. Don’t we? The fear of not having enough money is one of the issues most likely to trigger our deepest, most uncontrollable emotions or reflexes. Some say it triggers the amygdala—the part of our brain that controls our survival instincts. It’s where we choose to fight or flee. I’ve tried both of these options with my finances, and neither of them work very well. So, let’s instead have some fun with all of that gray area between fights and flights, or in your brain’s gray matter.

STEP FOUR: CREATE THE FUTURE.

I often listen to Jon Acuff’s Quitter audiobook while running or working out. It’s full of great tips for finding a job you enjoy more than your current one, and I’ve added some tips of my own below. Choose three actions from these, or create your own and get in action this week. You won’t get where you want to go by standing in place!

1.      Give 100% effort in your current job, including if your current job is to find a job.

2.      Take an inventory of what you do best and what makes you happiest.

3.      Review your personal or household budget for unnecessary expenses you could cut.

4.      Build up an emergency fund or savings account, so your job choices aren’t based solely on money desperation.

5.      Write a resume for the ideal candidate (you) in your dream job and determine where you might need to build some skills.

6.      Volunteer in a not-for-profit organization that will allow you to practice the skills you want to master.

7.      Accept an additional part-time job in a for-profit business that will allow you to practice the skills you want to master.

 

NOTE: For-profit businesses are probably violating Wage and Labor Laws if they offer to “hire” you as a volunteer. DO NOT work for anyone who refuses to at least pay you minimum wage for your services, even if they have to train you.

 

8.      Talk to your loved ones about your plans to leave your current job.

9.      Ask your loved ones how it will affect them and what concerns they have about a change.

10.  Listen fully to your loved ones and design a plan to minimze the risks they fear most.

11.  Search for a career counselor/coach or attend a job search event within your budget.

12.  Ask your current employer for extra work in an area you want to develop.

13.  Make a list of no less than 10 things you love about your current job.

14.  Discuss your “10 things” list with at least three people who you trust, who know you well and who see you more powerful than you see yourself.

It doesn’t always feel like it, but do you realize that you never technically have to do anything? This is not to say that your choice not to do something won’t cause a consequence you don’t like—or an unexpected one that you do and didn’t know was possible. It’s just very important for you to see that you are constantly making choices and most of them are very powerful. Some of them are like the ones you made as a child, but you make them differently now. You hated broccoli and Brussels sprouts before, but now you eat them because you’ve learned to cook them in new ways and you like the way your body feels when it’s properly nourished. You still choose ice cream for dinner sometimes. You just choose not to have the sugar crash, bloat and sluggishness that you can’t run off as easily as you did at five years old. You have too much you want to accomplish to at this stage of you life, so you opt for food that energizes you throughout the day. Hmmm. How is it that someone so powerful ended up with a financial obligation that weighs so heavily? Let’s take a look so you don’t repeat this cycle—and so that you can maybe eliminate this obligation.

1.      Determine the exact amount that you “have to” pay to meet this obligation you wish you didn’t have.

2.      Disclose the exact amount of this financial obligation with at least one person who you trust, who knows you well and who sees you more powerful than you see yourself.

3.      Make a list of all of the times you have had similar complaints about other obligations.

4.      Discuss your “10 things” list with at least three people who you trust, who know you well and who see you more powerful than you see yourself.

5.      Brainstorm five to 10 ways you can generate income to pay toward this obligation.

a)      Can you take a second or very part-time job?

b)      Can you find freelance assignments or odd jobs, such as on TaskRabbit?

c)      Can you have a yard, estate or other sale?

d)     Can you ask friends or family for a gift or loan?

e)      Can you take in a roommate? Or become one?

6.      Brainstorm five to 10 ways you could eliminate this obligation.

a)      Can you sell it or transfer ownership?

b)      Can you lease it part-time, such as on airbnb?

c)      Can you delegate it to someone else who benefits from it?

d)     Can you return it?

e)      Can you ask for loan forgiveness, reduction or other help?

7.      Add to your action plan some of the actions you brainstormed.

STEP FIVE: STAY ON PARR.

As you can probably see, this will be an ongoing process that includes changing the way you think about money. There is no “quick fix.” Even if you get a fast, desired result in a few actions, you are likely to repeat the pattern if you don’t keep working to repattern your brain responses. This is one of the toughest patterns to change, and you are not alone. However, you’ll forget that others have these issues, too. So, consider forming a group to help you stay on PARR. Together, you can plan, act, revise, and repeat until you get the results you want--consistently. You’ll also start to have a lot of fun talking about money powerfully, rather than fearing that it will come up in conversation.
